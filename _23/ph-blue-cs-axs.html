AXS Case Study

innit

Overview

Role and Duration

The project kicked-off early into my 4-year tenure at AXS and continued to my last days! As the sole UX/UI design resource in the UK, I was tasked by a Product Manager to improve the design of the Order Confirmation emails dispatched to customers following a ticket purchase.

Scope & Constraints

The original email templates were text-heavy with little branding; only a small header banner image distinguishing the visual identities of AXS’ main clients - The O2, Wembley Arena, AXS, and key promoters.

Branding aside, there was minimal formatting of text content, making it difficult for customers to find specific event or order details.

Project Summary

From the start, the new designs incorporated a flexible modular structure; splitting out event, ticket, order/payment, and venue details into sections. Text content within the sections was formatted and styled to convey hierarchy, thus improving clarity and readability.

The modular template was designed in such a way that it could be reskinned to match brand styles with minimal code alteration. An image featuring the brand/venue logo was embedded in the template, but the sets of colours used throughout the component structures were hard-coded, enabling visual brand identity - even if images were disabled.

The Project

Goals

Improve customer experience. Enhance brand identity for clients. Flexibility to accommodate template variations, specific event or venue requirements, multiple languages/currency/tax profiles, delivery methods, upsells, payment methods, ticket types. Reusable/adaptable modules. Consistent design and structure despite different data sources. Ability to create a suite of templates for new priority clients/brands/events.

Challenges

No availability of dev resource to implement the templates lack of knowledge/understanding of the email template files lack of buy-in/doubt that the designs could work. Must integrate with existing backend system data and infrastructure. Testing across multiple email clients without appropriate tools. Introduction of secondary backend system, which created entirely different datapoints. Had to accommodate both.

Having to learn xslt and conditional xml to meet relentless updates and requests. Lack of understanding/guardianship of template usage and branding, leading to hundreds of template variations. Template updates and documentation became impossible.

Client Services colleagues bypassing procedures and dismissing the communicated template restrictions, committing to client requests of non-templated content and design changes.

Process

Discovery

Competitor analysis Wireframing content analysis of order data

Design

simplifying brand identities into universal html elements

Using the available/required datapoints to form the structure of each section. Designing using familiar/known elements (event image; location/date icons. Ordering data from a customer perspective. Sections were iterated to accommodate specific requirements. Modular approach let this be undertaken independently of the other modules/content.

Prototyping the initial responsive email design as proof of concept, before further development incorporating actual system data.

Outcome

Results/Feedback

Clients liked it and began to request variations

The templates were rolled-out successfully. Testing was a challenge, however in the cases where the styling was not on-point, order/event details were always conveyed clearly.

Evolution

A number of enhancements & variations were added to the original standard suite of email templates, including:

  • Multi-event ticket orders
  • Multi-session ticket orders
  • Multiple ticket types & categories within same order
  • Season ticket orders
  • Named ticket assignees
  • Upgrade/Comp/VIP programme tickets
  • Ticket insurance add-on
  • English/Swedish localisation
  • Swedish tax breakdown
  • Pay with points payment method
  • Reservation payment prompts
  • Custom event-specific artwork

Learnings

Although template wires, structures and restrictions were documented in Confluence, it was rarely accessed. In hindsight, hands-on demo/Q&A sessions would have provided contextualisation and aided understanding for colleagues in non-technical roles.

Lack of support from the wider business led to significant time and resource wasted throughout the project.

Proposed backend system enhancements for events’ email/eticket setup (derived from research and feedback sessions with frustrated colleagues) were ultimately ignored by US-based management, despite approving the detailed and illustrated RFC.

Had the business provided appropriate development resource, the entire project would likely have been executed in at least half the time. The support of my Dev colleague was immeasurable, but relying on a designer to learn dev markup language on the job, relying on convoluted testing hacks and nervously attempting complex deployment procedures was wholly inefficient. And a fucking waste of time for everyone involved. Traumatic.
